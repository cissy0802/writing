BigCat's Writing

Intuition says "freedom" breeds creativity. The truth is usually the reverse: drop someone into a boundless blank and they seize up; hand them a topic, a word cap, a strange rule, and the sentences start pouring out. Today, a counterintuitive idea — constraint is not creativity's enemy but its engine. Four things: why limits give you freedom; how to start on a prompt instead of waiting for inspiration; how a word cap forces density; and the Oulipo, who turned constraint into a game.

Principle 01

Constraint as Engine: The More Limits, the More Freedom

Why a Narrower Problem Is an Easier One to Solve
Constraint · Stravinsky · Counterintuitive
The Principle in One Line

A blank canvas doesn't grant freedom — it grants paralysis. A constraint narrows infinite possibility into a problem you can actually start on — not "write something," but "write it with this word, in this length, under this rule." Once the choices are finite, attention shifts from "what to write" to "how to write it well."

"The more constraints one imposes, the more one frees oneself. And the arbitrariness of the constraint serves only to obtain precision of execution." — Igor Stravinsky, Poetics of Music (1942)
Why It Works

Psychologists call it the tyranny of choice: the more options, the more decision stalls. The blank page is the extreme case — anything goes, so nothing gets written. A constraint drags creativity from "diverging" back to "solving": give a topic, a rhyme, a word count, and the problem turns from open to closed — and closed problems are what the brain is good at. Constraint isn't a burden you tolerate; it's the fulcrum that gives the motion a shape.

Revision in Action
(Staring at a blank doc) Write something about "teamwork"… (half an hour later, still blank) Topic: In 300 words, use one real conflict from last week to show why async communication cut down misunderstandings; end on a question. — The moment the constraint gets specific, the first line arrives: "Last Wednesday, a colleague and I argued over an API for forty minutes, until someone said: write it down." Going from a "theme" to a "constrained assignment" is the step from paralysis to a first sentence.
Write something insightful about leadership. (blank page, 20 minutes gone) Constraint: In exactly 6 sentences, tell one time you gave a decision away and it went better than if you'd kept it. — The limit picks the story for you; the sentences start writing themselves.
When to Use It + Common Mistakes
  • ✓ When stuck, procrastinating, or facing a blank page: add three constraints, then write
  • ✓ Team brainstorms: "think under these three limits" beats "think freely" — more output, more focused
  • ✗ Trap: treating "free rein" as a gift to the writer — for most people it's a weight
  • ✗ Trap: constraints too loose ("write 500–5000 words, whatever") — that's no constraint at all
This Week's Exercise + A Question

Pick a topic you've long wanted to write but never started; give it three hard constraints (a word count, one required word, one forbidden word) and finish it today. Question: In your past "I couldn't write it" moments, were you short on inspiration — or short on constraint? If the latter, the problem was never you; the topic was just too big.

Principle 02

Writing to a Prompt: The Prompt Is a Gift, Not a Cage

Don't Wait for Inspiration — Say "Yes, And"
Prompt · Improv · Jack London
The Principle in One Line

Amateurs wait for inspiration; professionals show up. A prompt — an assigned topic, a daily writing cue, an editor's brief — isn't a constraint on creativity but a shortcut past the hardest step: starting from zero. Improv's first rule is "Yes, and": accept what you're handed, don't negate it, push it forward. Writing to a prompt is the same — accept the topic first, then see what it can grow into.

"You can't wait for inspiration. You have to go after it with a club." — commonly attributed to Jack London
Why It Works

Inspiration is a result, not a prerequisite. In neuroscience, action often precedes motivation — write first, and the state of writing gets summoned, not the other way round. A prompt's value is exactly this: it gives you the first handhold. The improv master Keith Johnstone found, in Impro, that the harder someone tries to "seem creative," the more they freeze; those who honestly accept a partner's offer and build on it are the ones who spark. Writing is the same — treat the prompt as a partner's opening line; your job isn't to judge whether it's good, but to "Yes, and" it onward. A prompt doesn't cap imagination; it gives it a starting block.

Revision in Action
(Handed the prompt "On perseverance") Too old, too broad, nothing worth saying… (gives up) Yes, and — accept "perseverance," then push to a specific angle: not "you should persevere," but "how I decide whether a thing is worth persevering with or worth quitting." Old prompt + one private angle = an essay only you can write. Negate the prompt and you leave empty-handed; accept it and add an angle, and you instantly have an entrance that's yours alone.
The assigned topic "teamwork" is boring, there's nothing new to say. Yes, and: keep "teamwork," but narrow to the moment it fails — write "The three sentences that quietly kill a team meeting." Same prompt, an angle only you have.
When to Use It + Common Mistakes
  • ✓ Assigned topics, newsletter columns, daily writing practice: treat the prompt as a starting block
  • ✓ When stuck, ask "Yes, and": what angle can I add to this topic that only I have?
  • ✗ Trap: spending more energy complaining about the prompt, or swapping it, than digging into it
  • ✗ Trap: waiting to "feel it" before writing — most of the time, writing is what produces the feeling
This Week's Exercise + A Question

Grab a "boring" broad topic (say, "time management") and use "Yes, and" to narrow it into a specific angle only you can write; give it 400 words. Question: To what extent is "waiting for inspiration" just a polite name for procrastination? If action precedes motivation, what needs fixing most — your mindset, or the friction of starting to type?

Principle 03

The Word Limit: Short Forces Density

A Cap Is the Most Honest Editor
Word count · Density · Strunk
The Principle in One Line

Give someone 5,000 words and they'll pad; give them 100 and they're forced to keep only what matters most. A word cap is the most honest editor there is — it won't argue with you, it just forces you to choose. Short doesn't mean less content; it means the filler is squeezed out and every word has to earn its place.

"Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences." — Strunk & White, The Elements of Style
Why It Works

Loose length indulges lazy thinking: if you can't think it through, just write a few more sentences and skate past. A word cap blocks that exit — to say a thing clearly in 100 words, you first have to think it clearly. That's why the six-word story, the drabble (a story of exactly 100 words), the telegram, the text message all sharpen the craft. The most-quoted example is "For sale: baby shoes, never worn." — six words, a whole tragedy. (Pinning it on Hemingway is almost certainly a later invention, but that doesn't dull what it proves: the bigger the blank, the heavier each word.) Capping the count is, at bottom, capping the room you have to dodge thinking.

Revision in Action
After a great deal of careful discussion and thorough evaluation, our team has ultimately reached the unanimous conclusion that this proposal is, under the present circumstances, a relatively viable option. (28 words) On review, this proposal is viable. (6 words) Cut to a fifth — no information lost. "Careful," "thorough," "unanimous," "relatively" never earned their place.
In order to arrive at a decision on this particular matter, we will need to take into consideration a number of different factors. (23 words) Deciding this means weighing several factors. (6 words) Down to a quarter. "In order to" and "a number of different" are water; "take into consideration" = weigh.
When to Use It + Common Mistakes
  • ✓ Subject lines, abstracts, elevator pitches, tweets, PR titles, commit messages: write long, then cut to the cap
  • ✓ Drill: halve the word count of any passage and see if it got stronger (it usually does)
  • ✗ Trap: mistaking "short" for "say less" — short is high density, not missing information
  • ✗ Trap: going vague to get short — concision presupposes clarity first (see the length discipline)
This Week's Exercise + A Question

Write a complete little story (with a turn) in exactly 100 words; then take a long email you sent recently and cut the body in half. Question: Why does writing short so often take longer than writing long? Pascal wrote, "I have made this letter longer because I have not had the time to make it shorter" — where, exactly, does the cost of short get paid?

Principle 04

Oulipo: Constraint as a Sentence-Making Machine

Invent a Rule, Then Solve Within It
Oulipo · Perec · Literary games
The Principle in One Line

In 1960, a group of French writers and mathematicians founded the Oulipo (the Workshop for Potential Literature), on a single creed: invent a strict formal rule for yourself in advance, then write within it — the constraint stops being an obstacle and becomes a machine that keeps spitting out new sentences. The stranger the rule, the more it forces expressions you'd otherwise never have reached.

"Oulipians: rats who build the labyrinth from which they plan to escape." — Raymond Queneau, co-founder of the Oulipo
Why It Works

The Oulipo pushes "constraint as engine" to its limit: don't wait for a constraint to appear — invent one on purpose as the motor of the work. Georges Perec wrote La Disparition (English: A Void), an entire novel without the letter e — the most common letter in French. This isn't showing off: forced to route around e, he had to reach for words and structures he'd never normally pick, and the language was wrung into new shapes. Queneau's Exercises in Style retells one trivial incident in 99 different styles — proof that "how you say it" is itself an infinite creative space. For engineers it's especially familiar: this is essentially "set a formal spec for the writing, then solve within the spec," isomorphic to writing constrained code.

Lipogramban a chosen letter throughout (Perec's whole novel avoids "e")
N+7replace each noun with the seventh noun after it in the dictionary
Snowballeach word is one letter longer than the last: I do not know…
Palindromereads the same forwards and backwards ("Was it a car or a cat I saw?")
Univocalicuse only a single vowel letter in the entire text
Common Oulipo constraints — the odder the rule, the harder it forces you off your defaults
Revision in Action
(Trying to praise a product as "fast," but only managing) Our product is very fast, really fast, super smooth to use. Self-set constraint: praise the speed in three sentences, but ban the word "fast." → "You click; before you blink, the result is already there." "We deleted the word 'wait' from the flow." — Ban a word, and out comes an image instead of an adjective. The practical value of an Oulipo-style constraint: block the lazy word, and you're forced into the concrete.
Our onboarding is simple and easy and user-friendly. Constraint: praise the onboarding without the words simple, easy, or friendly. → "New hires ship code before lunch on day one." Ban the lazy adjective and you're forced into evidence.
When to Use It + Common Mistakes
  • ✓ Breaking clichés: ban your favorite adjectives and force yourself into specifics
  • ✓ Creativity drills, team workshops, writing games for kids: constraint makes writing fun
  • ✓ Engineering analogy: treat writing as "solving within a formal spec" — the clearer the spec, the sharper the output
  • ✗ Trap: constraint for its own sake, spectacle over meaning — the constraint is a means; clarity is still the end
This Week's Exercise + A Question

Invent an Oulipo-style constraint for yourself (e.g., describe your job in 150 words without ever using the word "I") and write it. Question: After banning a word you lean on most, did your expression get worse — or get new? What does that tell you about how much of your usual "fluency" is really just habit?

— Going Deeper —
If constraint sparks creativity, is "total freedom" a myth in writing? Do we actually need it?
"Total freedom" is more a romantic inheritance than a real condition for making things. Most masterpieces were born inside tight constraints: the sonnet's strict meter, the haiku's 5-7-5, the classical couplet's exact parallelism, Hollywood's near-formulaic three acts. Constraint supplies two things freedom lacks — a starting point and resistance. Without a start, you freeze before the infinite; without resistance, thought skates the surface and refuses to go deep. Real freedom may not be "no constraints" but "constraints of your own choosing": you decide which shackles to wear, then push the motion within them to its limit.
In the AI era, has the meaning of "prompt" and "constraint" changed? Is writing a prompt for AI a new form of writing-to-a-prompt?
Highly isomorphic. Writing a prompt for AI is essentially "setting constraints on one generation" — role, format, length, exclusions, required points; the more precise the constraint, the more controllable the output. The flip side is more interesting: when AI can instantly produce fluent, unconstrained text, the scarce human value shifts precisely to "setting good constraints" — knowing what to limit, what to leave blank, what to want and what to refuse. A writer's moat in the AI era is no longer "putting words down" but "defining the problem, imposing constraints, judging the result." The skill of the prompt has been promoted from passively taking topics to actively setting them.
Word limits force density, but might they also force "short for short's sake" obscurity? Which is master, concision or clarity?
Clarity is master, concision the servant — and that order can't invert. The right use of a word limit is "make it clear, then cut the filler," not "cut it short enough and never mind whether it lands." Hemingway's telegraphic power works because what he omits is what the reader can supply themselves, leaving hooks behind; omit information the reader needs to understand, and short becomes mystification. The test is simple: after cutting, hand it to a fresh reader — if they stall, you cut muscle, not fat. Concision removes the redundant, not the substance — a discipline that matters most exactly when you're cut to the bone.
Do Oulipo's lipograms and palindromes — "word games" — actually help serious professional writing (memos, docs, pitches), or are they just fun?
The direct product (a lipogram itself) may not enter professional writing, but the muscle it trains is intensely practical. Banning a word forces you around your defaults into the concrete — precisely the ability memos and docs most lack (one fewer "we're committed to delivering an excellent experience," one more verifiable fact). Constraint also trains the "solve within a spec" mindset, isomorphic to engineers writing bounded systems: given the edges, make the solution optimal. Treat the Oulipo as a gym, not a stage — you're training the knack of routing around clichés straight to specifics, and once trained, your formal writing is naturally sharper. The game is the means; the skill is what stays.
