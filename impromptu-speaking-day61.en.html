Writing & Expression: Impromptu SpeakingThe 40 Seconds With No Script

BigCat's Writing

The exec who asks "how's the project going?" in the elevator. The all-hands where you're suddenly called on. The review where someone probes an angle you never prepared. What really defines your professional presence is rarely the rehearsed 40-minute keynote — it's these unscripted 40 seconds. The good news: impromptu speaking isn't a gift, it's a trainable craft. Four tools this week.

Principle 01

Carry a Structure — Turn the Essay Into a Fill-in-the-Blank

PREP & What / So What / Now What
Abrahams · Structure
The Principle

The great enemy of impromptu speaking isn't nerves — it's the wandering that comes from not knowing where to start. The cure isn't memorizing a script; it's carrying one or two ready-made frameworks. With a structure, the moment turns from "write an essay" into "fill in the blanks": your brain supplies content instead of inventing organization on the fly.

In the Master's Words
"Structure sets you free. When you know your structure, you can focus on your message and your audience rather than on what comes next." — Matt Abrahams, Think Faster, Talk Smarter (2023)
Why It Works

Working memory is a narrow pipe. When you speak off the cuff, it's already juggling content, emotion, and the faces in front of you. Ask it to also invent a structure and it overloads — you freeze, or you circle endlessly. A pre-loaded framework makes "organize" an automatic call, freeing every remaining cycle for content. The two most useful: PREP = Point → Reason → Example → Point; What? So What? Now What? = the fact → what it means → what to do. The first is for delivering a conclusion; the second for explaining why something matters.

(CEO in the elevator: "How's the Agent project going?") When to Use · Common Mistakes
  • ✓ Being called on to report, elevator Q&A, interview curveballs, off-the-cuff review remarks
  • ✗ Trying to memorize a full script — one break and it all collapses; impromptu wants a skeleton, not lines
  • ✗ Picking a framework too complex to recall live — two is plenty; drill them to reflex
  • ✗ Point with no Example sounds hollow; missing Now What leaves the audience without a next step
This Week's Exercise · A Question

Write PREP and What/So What/Now What each on a card; keep them in your wallet or phone notes. Any time you're asked a work question this week, force yourself to answer through one of them, even if it slows you a beat. Question: which skeleton fits the impromptu moments you meet most often — and why?

Principle 02

Buy Thinking Time — Make the Pause an Asset

The Power of the Purposeful Pause
Live · Buffering
The Principle

When you're caught, the two easy mistakes are filling the silence with "uh... um..." or blurting a bad answer. The skilled speaker takes a few seconds of quality pause to organize — and what the audience reads isn't "stuck," it's "thinking carefully." The pause is a privilege, not a flaw.

In the Master's Words
"The right word may be effective, but no word was ever as effective as a rightly timed pause." — Mark Twain
Why It Works

Silence is amplified three or four times in the speaker's own ears — you feel a long gap; the audience registers a fraction of a second. So your discomfort is not their awkwardness. Instead of leaking panic through filler, use three legitimate ways to buy time: ① restate or reframe the question ("So you're asking whether... right?") — it confirms and buys five seconds; ② unpack a key word ("'Scale' actually means two different things here..."); ③ an honest meta-comment ("That's worth thinking about for a second"). All three do work and read as professional. "Uh" does not.

Before & After
(Manager: "What's the biggest risk in this plan?") Uh... risk... hmm... let me think... maybe the timeline? The biggest risk — let me narrow the question first: are you more worried about technical risk or adoption risk? (Restate + counter-question: buys time and pins the scope) ...If it's adoption, it's the cross-team dependency.
Uh, the biggest risk, hmm, I guess... maybe the timeline? The biggest risk — let me make sure I answer the right one: are you asking about technical risk or adoption risk? ...If it's adoption, it's the cross-team dependency.
When to Use · Common Mistakes
  • ✓ High-pressure Q&A, interviews, board follow-ups, being cued to weigh in on the spot
  • ✗ Plugging the silence with filler (uh / um / like / you know)
  • ✗ Darting eyes and fidgeting during the pause — the silence didn't expose you, your body did
  • ✗ Restating until it becomes stalling; or opening with an apology ("sorry, I didn't prepare") — self-sabotage
This Week's Exercise · A Question

Record yourself answering three "ambush" questions and count the "uh"s and "um"s. On the next round, force yourself to replace every filler with a restatement of the question. Question: which filler word is your default — and what is it covering for: that you haven't thought it through, or that you fear the silence?

Principle 03

Interruptions & Hostile Questions: Acknowledge — Bridge — Communicate

The ABC of Not Getting Dragged Off Message
Live · Bridging
The Principle

A hostile question or an interruption is, at bottom, an attempt to drag you off your message. Bridging lets you politely acknowledge the other person, then pull the conversation back to the core you want to deliver — without stonewalling, and without being led away.

In the Master's Words
"Tactical empathy is understanding the feelings and mindset of another in the moment... It's about acknowledging their perspective, not necessarily agreeing with it." — Chris Voss, Never Split the Difference (2016)
Why It Works

Under attack, the two instincts — defensive stonewalling or caving and getting led away — both lose. The old crisis-comms formula, ABC, offers a third path: Acknowledge the legitimate kernel in their question (not surrender — just "I heard you") → Bridge with a transition ("What matters more is...") → Communicate, returning to your prepared core. Acknowledgment makes them feel heard and lowers the heat; the bridge leaves no vacuum for them to keep pressing. Same for interruptions: a brief acknowledgment ("Important point — I'm getting to it") then reclaim the rhythm.

When to Use · Common Mistakes
  • ✓ Review defenses, crisis comms, a challenged pitch, performance conversations, sharp client questions
  • ✗ Skipping the acknowledgment and bridging straight away — they feel ignored, and burn hotter
  • ✗ Over-acknowledging into an apology tour; or a clunky "but..." bridge that reads as dodging
  • ✗ No Communicate — acknowledging with nothing after it = surrender; talking over an interrupter reads as domineering — confirm, don't crush
This Week's Exercise · A Question

Recall a recent time you were challenged and fumbled the answer. Rewrite it with ABC in three sentences, and read it aloud. Question: the hardest part of Acknowledge is granting that they're partly right without conceding — can you find that honest, non-self-destructive pivot?

Principle 04

Impromptu Rests on Preparation: From "Performing" to "Communicating"

The Paradox — Great Improv Is Rehearsed Readiness
Mindset · Improv & Prep
The Principle

The most counterintuitive lesson: the best impromptu speakers are the best prepared. Impromptu doesn't mean "unprepared" — it means preparation internalized until it's available on demand, plus a mental switch that brings the anxiety down.

In the Master's Words
"It usually takes me more than three weeks to prepare a good impromptu speech." — widely attributed to Mark Twain
Why It Works

Two mechanisms. First, an internalized reserve: classical rhetoric already taught orators to stockpile examples, analogies, and lines (Latin copia, "abundance") so the moment is just retrieval — your "spontaneous" gem is usually old stock. Second, a reframe: Matt Abrahams notes that stage anxiety flows mostly from a "performance orientation" (everyone is judging me). Switch to a "communication orientation" (I'm helping this audience get something useful) and attention moves from self to other; the anxiety recedes. The companion move: don't aim for "brilliant," aim for "clear and good enough" — chasing perfection only amplifies the panic.

Before & After (rewriting the pre-stage inner monologue)
This is a disaster — everyone's watching, one slip and I'm done, I have to look impressive. They're not here to judge me — they want to know about this. My only job is to make it clear. Good enough is fine.
Everyone's judging me. I have to nail this and sound brilliant. They're not here to judge me — they want the information. My job is to make it clear, not perfect.
When to Use · Common Mistakes
  • ✓ The "warm-up" before any impromptu moment; the long build of impromptu capability
  • ✗ Romanticizing improv as "talent" and never training it — it's a muscle, not a bloodline
  • ✗ Sourcing content only in the moment, with no stockpile; chasing "dazzling" until you overload
  • ✗ Treating nerves as an enemy to suppress — the harder you press, the tighter you get; relabel the energy as "I'm engaged"
This Week's Exercise · A Question

Build an "ammunition store": collect 10 reusable analogies / figures / short stories from your field, and drill each until you can tell it in 30 seconds. This is the reserve behind every future impromptu. Question: next time you're nervous, swap the inner "I'm so anxious" for "I'm so engaged," and watch your body — does the feeling follow the label?

— Going Deeper —
Improv and preparation seem opposed, yet this issue says "impromptu rests on preparation." So where does "presence" really come from — being unprepared, or being prepared to the point of invisibility?
Real presence comes from preparation so thorough it leaves no trace. The audience feels "natural" precisely because you're not distracted tuning structure or hunting for an example — they're already reflex. A jazz musician drilled scales for a thousand hours before improvising; a stand-up's "ad lib" sits on a mountain of stored material. What we call talent is mostly practice hidden well enough. Unprepared improv isn't freedom — it's exposure: no skeleton, no ammunition, just anxiety amplified. The paradox: the more you prepare for the impromptu, the more it looks like you were born able to speak. The scarce thing was never nerve to open your mouth — it's having something ready when you do.
AI can feed you answers in real time (an earpiece prompter). Doesn't that devalue the skill of impromptu speaking?
What gets devalued is memorizing an answer; what doesn't is reading the room. AI can supply content, structure, and phrasing — but the core of impromptu lives exactly where a prompter can't reach: sensing the doubt on the face in front of you, hearing the real concern behind the question, deciding in half a second whether to push or soften, catching the moment with an example only someone present would get. Those are the abilities of being there, not of retrieval. The more fluent AI's generated content becomes, the more the human moat shifts toward real-time social judgment and adaptation. The skilled improviser isn't replaced by AI — their reserve just gained a faster assistant.
How does impromptu differ across languages? Would a Western skeleton like PREP — "lead with your point" — feel abrupt when delivered aloud in a high-context culture?
The skeleton transfers; the register must be localized. High-context conversation tends to set up relationship and background before the conclusion, so an abrupt "My point is..." can read as pushy in some formal settings. The fix isn't to abandon PREP but to add a softening lead-in — "I have a rough thought on this," then the Point, or turning the closing restatement into an invitation, "I wonder if that holds up." In lower-context, English-speaking rooms it's the reverse: too much setup reads as evasive or unsure, and getting to the point looks professional. Same skeleton — wrapped in deference and relationship in one culture, stripped to the facts in another. What localizes is the shell, not the logic.
How do you calibrate "acknowledge" (the A in ABC) when power is unequal — a junior being needled by a senior? Where does professional end and weak begin?
Separate acknowledging a fact from acknowledging your worth. Granting a specific fact they raised ("yes, that number did lag") is professional — it rests on shared ground and makes you look steady and honest. Acknowledging all the way to "it's my fault, blame me" is weakness: it slides the discussion from problem to penance. The pivot: acknowledgment must be followed by Bridge and Communicate — you caught the heat, then immediately steer back to something actionable. When power is unequal, the strongest posture is neither firing back nor lying down, but "I hear your concern, and here's how I plan to handle it." Acknowledgment buys the right to keep being heard — not the signal of surrender.
