BigCat's Writing

Joan Didion was a founder of American New Journalism and one of the most exacting prose stylists in English. Forget the legend for a day; study the craft — how a cold, unhurried eye lets a detail carry its own verdict, how a single "I" becomes an instrument for measuring an era, how moving one word can change a sentence the way moving a camera changes what it shoots, and how she turned reported fact into something that reads like poetry.

Principle 01

The Cool Eye: Let the Detail Carry the Verdict

Report the concrete; withhold the adjective
Didion · Observation · Restraint
The Principle in One Line

Didion never tells you what to feel. She sets the most precise, most piercing concrete detail in front of you and walks away — unhurried, unjudging record cuts deeper than any adjective.

"Writers are always selling somebody out." — Joan Didion, Preface to Slouching Towards Bethlehem (1968)
Why It Works

That line names Didion's stance: cold, honest, unsparing. Writing about the hippies of 1967 San Francisco, she doesn't announce "it was a decadent, chaotic age." She calmly records a five-year-old girl, dosed with LSD by her mother, sitting on the floor with a picture book. Not one word of judgment — and the image levels you. An adjective is a conclusion the writer reaches for the reader; a detail lets the reader reach it alone — and only the second one stays. Same for engineers: rather than "the system is unstable," give "it went down four times last week." Numbers and images carry their own verdict. You don't have to shout it.

Revision in Action
Morale on the team was terribly low last quarter and everyone had lost faith in the project. Last quarter the standup ran forty minutes. By the third week, four cameras stayed on. The first is three hollow verdicts you forget instantly. The second judges nothing — yet "morale collapsed" arrives on its own. That is the detail speaking for you.
这个季度团队士气非常低落，大家都对项目失去了信心。 这个季度，站会从九点开到九点四十；到第三周，只剩四个人还开着摄像头。 Delete the judgment words; keep only the verifiable picture — and the reader believes it more deeply, in either language.
When to Use It + Common Traps
  • ✓ Retros, incident reports, observational essays, case studies — anywhere a conclusion must be earned, not injected
  • ✓ Self-check: circle every adjective and ask, "can this be a concrete fact instead?"
  • ✗ Trap: inflating with big words ("catastrophic," "unprecedented") — with no detail underneath, it reads as bluff
  • ✗ Trap: cold to the point of coldness — pure objectivity chills too; under Didion's cool there is always care
This Week's Exercise + A Question

Describe a team or life scene you observed recently. Rule: not a single adjective — only what a camera could film. Let the reader reach your judgment alone. Question: "unhurried" and "cold" are one line apart. Where is that line?

Principle 02

Self & Era: Make "I" the Instrument That Measures the Age

Read a whole era through one honest pair of eyes
Didion · Perspective · Narrative
The Principle in One Line

Didion doesn't fake objectivity. She turns "I" — my insomnia, my migraines, the fragments I saw — into an instrument for taking the era's measurement. The more honestly you write one specific self, the more of a whole age it reflects.

"We tell ourselves stories in order to live." — Joan Didion, opening of The White Album (1979)
Why It Works

Right after that famous line she writes: "I was meant to know the plot, but all I knew was what I saw: flash pictures in variable sequence, images with no 'meaning' beyond their temporary arrangement." She admits she can't grasp the era's "plot" — only fragments. So she writes the act of selecting and arranging those fragments openly into the piece: narrative isn't objective truth, it's the version that "holds together," the one we choose out of chaos in order to keep going. A crucial reminder for leaders: the "company story" you tell your team is also a selection and an arrangement of countless facts. Admitting that is stronger than pretending it's the only truth.

Chaotic fragments (what I saw) → Select · Arrange → A story that holds
"We tell ourselves stories in order to live" — narrative is a workable version chosen from chaos, not truth itself
Revision in Action
The industry is undergoing a profound transformation that everyone must adapt to. That winter, three people I knew were laid off in one week; for the first time the job posts read "AI fluency preferred." The first is a grand verdict anyone could utter — empty. The second uses three small things "I" saw, and "the era changed" surfaces on its own. The personal is the evidence.
这个行业正在经历深刻的变革，每个人都必须适应。 那年冬天，我认识的三个人在同一周被裁；招聘启事里第一次写着「熟练使用 AI 者优先」。 Reduce "the era" to "what I actually saw this one year," and the abstraction gains weight at once.
When to Use It + Common Traps
  • ✓ Opinion pieces, industry observation, personal-brand essays, vision talks — when you want the feel of an era without shouting slogans
  • ✓ Replace "everyone is…" and "in this age…" with "three things I saw" — anchor the grand in the specific
  • ✗ Trap: the personal slides into the narcissistic — all "me, me, me," forgetting "I" is a mirror aimed at the age
  • ✗ Trap: passing one view off as the era's truth, forgetting it's only "the version you chose"
This Week's Exercise + A Question

Use "the self as instrument" to write about a current trend (AI, layoffs, remote work). Rule: no macro verdicts — only three specific fragments you saw or lived firsthand. Question: a personal view is inherently narrow. How do you use the truth of "I" without passing one view off as the whole picture of an age?

Principle 03

Sentence Precision: Word Order Is Meaning, Grammar Is a Stance

Move a word the way you move a camera
Didion · Syntax · Precision
The Principle in One Line

Didion said grammar was a piano she played by ear — but no one knew its power better: shift the position of one word and the sentence's meaning shifts, the way moving a camera changes the meaning of what it shoots.

"To shift the structure of a sentence alters the meaning of that sentence, as definitely and inflexibly as the position of a camera alters the meaning of the object photographed." — Joan Didion, Why I Write (1976)
Why It Works

Didion's sentences are famous for precision: every word's position is calculated, above all at the end. English puts its stress at the tail of a sentence, so the most important word belongs last. Chinese lacks English inflection, but word order, where you break the sentence, and what you leave for the end decide what the reader sees first and remembers last. Bury "I was wrong" in a subordinate clause, or stand it alone at the end — the tone and the accountability are worlds apart. What writers most often ignore is exactly this "morality of arrangement": where you place responsibility, where you place emphasis, exposes your stance.

Revision in Action
I was wrong, though admittedly the data was incomplete at the time. The data was incomplete. I was wrong. Same content. The first tucks "I was wrong" up front, softened by "though" — it reads like dodging. The second lets "I was wrong" stand alone at the end, in the stress position. Reorder, and the accountability lands.
我错了，虽然当时的信息确实不太完整。 当时信息不完整。但我错了。 In Chinese the lever is the break, not inflection: cut "I was wrong" into its own final clause and it gains all the weight.
When to Use It + Common Traps
  • ✓ Any writing where the point must land: conclusion sentences, apologies, commitments, one-liners, the close of a talk
  • ✓ When editing, ask: is the most important word sitting at the end — or buried in the middle?
  • ✗ Trap: stuffing the key fact into a subordinate clause, softening with "although" — the reader can't find what you meant
  • ✗ Trap: assuming long sentences read as rigorous — usually they just dilute the point
This Week's Exercise + A Question

Take an important sentence you wrote recently (a promise, a judgment). Move its single most important word to the end, standing alone, and compare the change in tone. Question: Chinese has no end-stress tradition like English. What carries "put the point last" in Chinese — the break, the short clause, or something else?

Principle 04

The Poetry of Fact: Repetition, Rhythm, Restraint

Turn reported fact into something that reads like a poem
Didion · Rhythm · Poetry
The Principle in One Line

Didion writes fact, yet it reads like poetry. The secret is rhythm: short sentences, repetition, the sudden stop. One word repeated three times hits harder than three different adjectives.

"Life changes fast. Life changes in the instant. You sit down to dinner and life as you know it ends." — Joan Didion, The Year of Magical Thinking (2005)
Why It Works

That is the opening of her book on her husband's sudden death. Not one ornate word — all of them plain — yet where does the blow come from? From the repetition of "Life changes," from the stop-and-start beat of short sentences, from "you sit down to dinner," an act so ordinary it disarms you, followed by "life as you know it ends." The poetry isn't in the vocabulary; it's in rhythm and restraint — repetition builds weight, short sentences build pause, a small concrete act grounds an abstract grief. This craft transfers cleanly into incident retros and product narratives: to make a line stick, don't add adjectives — tune the rhythm.

Revision in Action
The outage was serious and affected many users; we must take it seriously. Checkout was down. Down for forty-seven minutes. For forty-seven minutes, nothing sold. The first piles three big words together — blunt. The second uses the repetition of "down" and "forty-seven minutes," the pause of short sentences, to drive the severity home — with no adjective at all.
这次故障影响非常严重，大量用户受到波及。 支付挂了。挂了四十七分钟。那四十七分钟里，没有一笔订单成交。 Repeat the key word, cut the long sentence into short ones — the rhythm itself becomes the argument, in either language.
When to Use It + Common Traps
  • ✓ Lines that must be remembered: the first sentence of an incident retro, the close of a talk, the climax of a brand story
  • ✓ To emphasize, don't add adjectives — try repeating the key word, chopping the long sentence into short ones
  • ✗ Trap: mistaking "forceful" for "straining" — pile on big words and the sentence only grows blunter
  • ✗ Trap: overusing repetition until every line is parallel — the rhythm turns from poem to slogan
This Week's Exercise + A Question

Pick something you want remembered (an outage, a decision). Write it "normally," then rewrite: short sentences only, repeat one key word at least once, no adjectives. Read both aloud; hear which one is fiercer. Question: repetition and short sentences create force, but overused they read as sloganeering. What tells you where the "poem vs. slogan" line falls?

— Going Deeper —
Does Didion's "cold" still hold in business writing that needs empathy — or does it read as cold-heartedness?
Her cold isn't cold-heartedness; it's restraint — care underneath, simply not shouted. The difference: coldness doesn't care; restraint cares too much to let cheap sentiment cheapen it. Bad news, layoffs, incident notices need exactly this — precise fact in place of florid apology, and the reader feels your sincerity more. Truly cold writing is the kind too lazy to even give the detail.
Using "I" as an instrument to read an era — how do you avoid passing one view off as the era's truth?
Didion's own answer: confess you have only fragments. She never says "this is the era"; she says "this is what I saw." Marking the limits of your vantage honestly — "as I saw it," "among the people I knew" — keeps the real weight of personal experience while refusing the universal claim. Admitting narrative is "the version I chose" is more credible than faking objectivity.
How does "grammar is meaning" hold in Chinese, which lacks English end-stress?
Chinese leans not on inflection but on the break and the ordering. Where English loads the tail stress, Chinese emphasizes by standing a clause alone and placing it last: cut the heaviest words into their own final sentence and they gain weight. Classical parallelism and the staccato of modern short clauses are the Chinese "morality of arrangement." Word order is a stance in any language.
The poetry of fact vs. the clarity of a memo: when do you put the literature away and just state facts?
It depends on the goal. Want them to "act" — a decision memo, an operating guide — put the rhythm away: conclusion first, zero ambiguity; poetry only muddies it. Want them "moved and remembering" — the opening of a retro, the close of a vision talk — then reach for repetition and short sentences. Masters often layer within one piece: clarity carries the body, and only on the one or two lines that must land do they bring out Didion's beat.
