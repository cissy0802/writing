BigCat's Writing

Every sentence you write carries a layer of cultural default you never notice — how much meaning goes into the literal words, how blunt criticism should be, whether a joke or a metaphor can cross a language border. Inside one culture those defaults are invisible air. But the moment your reader comes from another context, the same sentence gets read as something entirely different. For a leader expressing themselves to a global team in two languages, cross-cultural writing isn't diplomacy — it's whether your message arrived at all. Four principles today.

Principle 01

High vs Low Context: First Figure Out Where the Meaning Lives

Where the Meaning Lives — Words, or Everything Around Them
Context · Making It Explicit
The Principle in One Line

Cross-cultural communication often fails not because too little was said, but because the two sides disagree on where the meaning belongs. High-context cultures keep meaning outside the words — in the relationship, the setting, the tone, the unsaid. Low-context cultures put all of it into the words. Step one is to spot the lowest-context reader in the room and write for them.

"A high-context communication is one in which most of the information is either in the physical context or internalized in the person, while very little is in the coded, explicit, transmitted part of the message." — Edward T. Hall, Beyond Culture (1976)

Hall gave us the single most useful cultural axis there is: how much of the message rides in the words themselves versus in everything around them.

Why It Works

Chinese and Japanese lean high-context, where "leaving it unsaid" and "you know what I mean" are marks of tact; German and American business English lean low-context, where "spell it out, write it down, don't make me guess" is what counts as responsible. The same hedged email reads to a Chinese colleague as "he doesn't really approve," and to a German colleague as a plain "OK." The misfire isn't in the language — it's in the mismatch of context level. Writing for a mixed audience, default toward low context: drag the subtext out of the air and put it on the page. Not to talk down to anyone, but to keep understanding from depending on a background you don't actually share.

Revision in Action
(hedged, to a German colleague) We should probably revisit the launch timing at some point. I recommend delaying launch by two weeks, because X isn't tested yet. If you'd rather keep the original date, let's settle it by Friday. (the "I disagree" is pulled out of the subtext and made into an actionable line)
Let me know your thoughts whenever. Please reply with a yes/no on the two-week delay by Thursday.
When to Use It + Common Mistakes
  • ✓ Cross-border email, global announcements, remote async work — writing for people whose faces you can't see
  • ✗ Assuming "I was perfectly clear" — clear only inside your own context
  • ✗ The reverse: being so blunt with a high-context reader that you come off as rude; direction depends on who's across from you
  • ✗ Leaving the key decision in the "between the lines," hoping they'll infer it
This Week's Exercise + A Question

Pull up an email you sent a cross-cultural colleague and ask of each line: "Is any meaning here riding on subtext?" Rewrite every such spot into an explicit, literal statement. Question: does saying everything flat and full sacrifice the grace and slack of high-context communication? When is leaving room the higher form of clarity?

Principle 02

Direct vs Indirect: The Same Criticism Sours One Culture Over

Calibrating How Criticism Travels
Feedback · Calibrating Intensity
The Principle in One Line

When you give feedback, "how direct is appropriate" is set by culture, not universal. Some cultures amp criticism up with words like absolutely and totally; others soften it with a little and sort of. You speak on your scale, they hear on theirs, and the error opens up right there. The craft of cross-cultural feedback is to mark your intensity explicitly.

"In some cultures negative feedback is given with upgrader words like absolutely or totally; in others it is softened with downgrader words like kind of, sort of, a little." — Erin Meyer, The Culture Map (2014)

Meyer draws this as a full axis running from direct to indirect — and warns that where you sit on it is nearly invisible to you and glaring to everyone else.

Why It Works

A Dutch or German leader saying "this doesn't work" is usually just addressing the work; a British, Chinese, or Japanese leader saying "that's interesting, maybe take another look" may already be a heavy no. The danger fires in both directions: direct people wound indirect readers without noticing, and indirect people are so soft that direct readers never register "this is a problem." The fix isn't picking a side — it's separating "how serious this is" from "how I say it." The tone can be gentle; the severity must be nailed down.

Soft words · direct ears
You say "maybe relook at the intro," meaning "this must be rewritten." Your direct-culture report hears "optional suggestion" — and changes nothing. Your tact became their "all good."
Blunt words · indirect ears
You say "this section's logic is wrong," meaning just the work. Your high-context colleague hears "a public rejection of me" — and holds a grudge. Your candor became their humiliation.
Both directions distort — the fix isn't to get blunter or softer, but to state explicitly whether this is a must-fix or a nice-to-have
Revision in Action
This is fine, I guess. Maybe take another look at the intro. Two things work well: X and Y. One must change before we ship — the intro buries the ask; move it to line one. To be clear, this is a blocker, not a nit. (severity nailed down: which line is "must," which is "optional")
(in front of everyone, to a face-conscious colleague) The logic in this section is wrong. (privately, as a question) I lost the thread of your conclusion here — is a step missing in the middle? Could you fill it in? (tone softened, but "there's a problem, it needs fixing" left in no doubt)
When to Use It + Common Mistakes
  • ✓ Cross-cultural code review, feedback to global reports, performance conversations
  • ✗ Treating "direct = honest, indirect = insincere" as universal truth — that's just your cultural preference
  • ✗ Downgrading a blocker to a nit to save face; they don't fix it, and that's on you
  • ✗ Adjusting only the tone, never the severity: so polite they can't tell it's a must-fix
This Week's Exercise + A Question

Write a piece of feedback for a cross-cultural report, split explicitly into two columns: what's a must-fix (blocker) and what's optional (nit), then wrap each in a tone that culture can accept. Question: on a team whose bluntness is the opposite of your home culture's, should you adapt your style to fit in, or hold your own and let others adjust to you? What does each cost?

Principle 03

International English: Writing for People Whose Second Language It Is

International English — Writing for the Non-Native Reader
Global English · Clarity
The Principle in One Line

Most readers in a global audience speak English as a second language. Writing "international" English isn't writing more childishly — it's cutting the things only native speakers get instantly: idioms, slang, sports and pop-culture references, roundabout phrasal verbs, so the meaning doesn't lean on a shared background you don't have.

"辞達而已矣。" — "Words that convey the meaning suffice." — Confucius, The Analects, Book 15

Twenty-five centuries early, that line is the yardstick for international English: the goal is to convey, not to show off. Orwell's sixth-sense rule agrees — if there's an everyday word, never reach for the fancy foreign one.

Why It Works

Native speakers routinely overestimate shared context. "Let's touch base and circle back once we move the needle" is a triple obstacle for a non-native reader: touch base (baseball), circle back (business jargon), move the needle (a gauge metaphor). Clear international English isn't dumbing down — it's lifting the cognitive load off the reader: short sentences, common words, explicit subjects, one word one meaning, and spell out an acronym on first use. It reads better for native speakers too — nobody ever complained a sentence was too clear. (Jean-Paul Nerrière nicknamed this stripped-down English "Globish.")

Revision in Action
Let's touch base offline and circle back once we've moved the needle on the low-hanging fruit. Let's talk this week. Once we finish the two easy fixes, I'll email an update. (all three idioms swapped for words anyone reads at a glance)
Please do the needful and revert back to me asap. (a localized, redundant phrasing others may not parse) Please review the document and reply by Thursday. (clear action, clear deadline, no slang)
When to Use It + Common Mistakes
  • ✓ Global RFCs, cross-border Slack, docs and talks for multilingual readers, international pitches
  • ✗ Showing off with idioms, puns, and sports references — native speakers laugh, everyone else goes blank
  • ✗ Mistaking "international English" for broken English: it wants clarity, not damage
  • ✗ Leaving acronyms and inside jargon unexplained, assuming the world knows your team's shorthand
This Week's Exercise + A Question

Take a piece of your English writing, circle every idiom, phrasal verb, cultural reference, and acronym, and swap each for something a non-native reader gets on first read. Question: does stripping out idioms and references wash the language into something bland and characterless? Can clarity and flavor coexist — and how?

Principle 04

Cultural Taboos: Your Defaults Aren't the World's Defaults

Your Defaults Aren't Universal
Checking Defaults · Avoiding Offense
The Principle in One Line

The most dangerous cultural assumptions are the ones you never registered as assumptions — a joke, a metaphor, a holiday "everyone" celebrates, a gesture, a color. Before writing for a cross-cultural audience, ask one thing: right here, am I treating "common sense where I'm from" as "common sense for humans"?

"The most obvious, important realities are often the ones that are hardest to see and talk about." — David Foster Wallace, This Is Water (Kenyon College, 2005)

Your own culture is the water — the thing you're swimming in and can't see. The taboo you'd never notice is the one someone else spots on the first read.

Why It Works

Taboos come in two kinds. One offends: jokes about religion, politics, a "happy holidays" that assumes Christmas, color symbolism (white is mourning in East Asia, weddings in the West). The other doesn't offend but simply fails: local sports and TV references, historical analogies only your country gets, defaulting to Fahrenheit, miles, your currency, your date format. The fix isn't memorizing a taboo list — you can't finish it — but building the reflex of "is this my water?", and, when you can, having one outsider read it first. The defaults you can't see, others catch instantly.

Revision in Action
(global announcement) Hope everyone had a great holiday season and is refreshed for Q1! (assumes Christmas + a Western fiscal calendar) As many of you return from different holidays this month, welcome back. Here's what's ahead this quarter. (assumes no single shared holiday)
(rallying an international team) This project is our Normandy landing. (a local historical reference that lands for some readers and misfires for others) This project is our most important fight this year — the biggest bet, the highest risk. (keeps the weight of "decisive battle," drops the reference only part of the room shares)
When to Use It + Common Mistakes
  • ✓ Global announcements, cross-border pitches, talks and training material for diverse readers
  • ✗ Using references and analogies only your own country gets, and thinking they're vivid
  • ✗ Opening "light" with religion, politics, or body humor
  • ✗ Defaulting units, date formats (US MM/DD vs. the rest's DD/MM), and name order
This Week's Exercise + A Question

Take something you wrote for a global audience and mark every "default" — holidays, units, metaphors, references, colors, date formats — and rewrite the ones that depend on local background into neutral phrasing. Question: strip out every culture-specific metaphor and reference and does the writing lose its concreteness and life? How do you trade off "universally legible" against "rooted and alive"?

— Deeper Questions —
Does chasing "low context, say it all" sacrifice the grace of high-context communication? When should subtext stay?
Explicitness is insurance against misreading, not a creed of "the barer the better." Wherever a misread does damage — decisions, commitments, deadlines, who's responsible — say it full. But when you're building a relationship, showing respect, or leaving someone room, the blank space is the content, and spelling it out reads as rude. Skilled operators layer it: facts and action items in explicit low context, the interpersonal and emotional left a degree of high-context room. The test isn't "which is more sophisticated," but "what happens here if it's misread."
Cutting idioms and references for "international English" — does it wash the language flat and characterless? Can clarity and flavor coexist?
They can — you just source the flavor elsewhere. Idioms and local references are borrowed vividness: they depend on a reader's shared memory and die at the border. Genuinely cross-cultural style comes from other places: exact verbs, concrete nouns, sentence rhythm, images anyone can feel (hunger, light, a door, a river). Cutting "move the needle" doesn't cut vividness — it forces you to be vivid without inside knowledge. Every ounce of force then comes from the meaning itself, not a shared password.
Do Chinese and English sit at the two ends of the high/low-context scale? Why does word-for-word translation so often satisfy neither?
Broadly yes: Chinese leans on parataxis and negative space, with the weight often outside the words; English (business English especially) leans on explicitness and hypotaxis, putting the logical connectors on the table. Literal translation is neither clear nor graceful because it moves the words but not "where the meaning belongs." Turns and emphases that Chinese carries through word order and tone must become explicit however / must in English; drag English bluntness word-for-word into Chinese and it reads harsh and impolite. Good translation isn't swapping words — it's switching context level.
AI translation crosses languages — can it cross context and taboo? Does it flatten high-context subtlety, or carry a taboo straight over?
For now it crosses language far better than context. It stumbles in two places: it flattens high-context subtlety — dropping the between-the-lines or over-explicitly spelling it out, both distortions; and it doesn't know who your reader is, so a metaphor, holiday default, or color symbol that's harmless in the source gets carried straight over, because it's translating words, not occasions. So AI is a fine first-draft engine, but "is this appropriate for these readers, does it cross a line" still needs a human who knows both cultures. Use it as a translator, not a cultural advisor.
