Writing & Expression: Writing for Children & TeensAge-Appropriate Language · Wisdom Without Preaching · Picture-Book Text · Reverse-Engineering the Classics

BigCat's Writing

Writing for children is the most merciless training there is. A child won't pretend to understand, won't read on out of politeness—one clumsy line and they turn the page and leave. Writing for the toughest reader alive builds the core of all good writing: radical clarity, concrete images, respect for the reader. The lesson isn't "childishness"—it's making the complex both precise and warm. The craft that reaches a child reaches a boardroom too.

Principle 01

Write Up, Not Down: Match the Reader's Stage

Age-Appropriate Language — Register, Not Dumbing Down
Reader Tiers · Register
The Principle

A five-, nine-, and fourteen-year-old are three different readers: different vocabularies, different sentence capacity, different amounts of abstraction they can hold. Matching age is not "making it shallow"—it's choosing register with precision: words and sentences this age can actually chew, delivering the real thing intact. E.B. White put it bluntly: to write down to children is a waste of time. You have to write up—treat the child as a sharp, demanding reader worth your best.

In the Master's Words
"Anyone who writes down to children is simply wasting his time. You have to write up, not down. Children are demanding. They are the most attentive, curious, eager, observant, sensitive, quick, and generally congenial readers on earth." — E.B. White, The Paris Review interview (1969)

White wrote Charlotte's Web and Stuart Little—and co-wrote The Elements of Style. He believed the discipline was the same for a child and for a Supreme Court justice: never condescend.

Why It Works

Why does "writing down" fail? Because it mistakes simple words for simple thoughts—underrating the child's mind while poisoning the prose with baby-talk. True age-matching swaps the shell, not the core: the weight of the idea stays; you just pour it into a vessel this age can reach—short sentences, concrete nouns, images you can see. This transfers to work in one step: writing for engineers versus a board isn't about "depth," it's about register. The office translation of White's rule: don't spin the exec as if she were an outsider—make the complex land in one hearing. That's respect.

Before → After
(Explaining to a six-year-old why the sky is blue) Atmospheric molecules produce stronger Rayleigh scattering of shorter-wavelength blue light. Sunlight is really all the colors hiding together. The air up there loves to grab the blue and fling it everywhere. So when you look up—your eyes are full of flung-around blue.
The nocturnal predator utilizes echolocation to navigate in the absence of light. At night the bat can't see. So it shouts—and listens for the shout to bounce back. That's how it finds its way in the dark.
Where to Use It · Common Mistakes
  • ✓ Graded readers, popular science, teaching—anywhere you deliver the real thing to someone without the background yet
  • ✓ Transfer: explaining tech to non-experts, a one-pager for execs, an onboarding doc for new hires
  • Trap: treating "simple words" as "simple thoughts," gutting the content until only correct filler remains
  • Trap: cutesy, sing-song register ("the itty-bitty sun shines warm-warm")—sticky, not clear; children hate it too
This Week's Exercise · A Question

Take your most technical concept (distributed consensus, options, attention mechanisms—your pick) and write two versions: one for a six-year-old, one for a smart but non-expert exec. Concrete images only, no jargon. Question: Is the difference between them "depth" or "register"? What survived in both versions—that may be the concept's true core.

Principle 02

Wisdom Without Preaching: Let the Moral Grow from the Story

Dissolve the Moral — Never Preach It
Values · Show, Don't Sermon
The Principle

Children—like adults—instinctively resist being lectured. The moment you post "The lesson of this story is…," their defenses go up and the point can't get in. The best children's books never sermonize: the value lives inside the character's choices and the story's turn, and the reader arrives at it themselves. C.S. Lewis's warning: don't write a story to inject a moral—let the images grow their own; and if they grow none, don't force one in.

In the Master's Words
"Let the pictures tell you their own moral. For the moral inherent in them will rise from whatever spiritual roots you have succeeded in striking during the whole course of your life. But if they don't show you any moral, don't put one in." — C.S. Lewis, "Sometimes Fairy Stories May Say Best What's to Be Said" (1956)

Lewis wrote the Narnia books. His point: a moral you bolt on stays outside the reader; one that grows from the story becomes part of them.

Why It Works

Preaching triggers reactance: the instant a person feels ordered how to think, their first move is to push back—even when the point is right. A value that's experienced—through a character's pain, choice, cost—slips past the defenses and gets taken as the reader's own discovery. This is exactly the "Emotional" and "Story" machinery in Made to Stick: what moves people isn't the conclusion, it's the passage there. It cuts just as sharply in leadership writing: instead of "we value ownership" in an all-hands note, tell the true story of someone who stayed up carrying an outage—no slogan, and they remember.

Before → After
The bear fell many times but never gave up, and finally learned to ride. The lesson: always persevere—persistence is victory. The bear crashed into the grass for the seventh time. It lay still, smelling the dirt. Then it stood the bike back up and pushed it to the top of the hill. "Again." (Not one word of moral—the reader got it anyway.)
The lesson of this story is that we should always be honest and never tell lies. Sam looked at the broken cup, then at his mother's face. He could feel the lie sitting ready in his mouth. He swallowed it. "I did it," he said—and watched her face change.
Where to Use It · Common Mistakes
  • ✓ Stories, talks, all-hands notes, brand narrative—anywhere you transmit a value rather than issue an order
  • ✓ Transfer: replace slogans with real cases, replace a "company value" with one person's choice
  • Trap: tacking a moral onto the ending ("this shows us…"), flattening the feeling the reader just built
  • Trap: the protagonist becomes a mouthpiece—existing only to voice what the author wants said, never a real person
This Week's Exercise · A Question

Find a piece of "moral" writing you've done—a family letter, a retro, a note to your team. Cross out every sentence that states the lesson outright; keep only the concrete scenes, actions, dialogue. Reread: is the point still there? If it's gone, it was only said, never shown. Question: Does giving no conclusion at all risk letting the value miss? Is "don't preach" in tension with a memo that must state its recommendation?

Principle 03

Picture-Book Text: Word and Image Each Carry Half

Picture-Book Text — Word and Image in Counterpoint
Counterpoint · Economy
The Principle

In a picture book, word and image each shoulder half the load and never repeat the other. Whatever the picture can show, the text shouldn't describe—if the bear is already crying on the page, "the bear cried" wastes half the spread and insults the reader's eyes. The text does what the picture can't: name the unseen feeling, the suspense of the next page, the passage of time. The page turn is the beat—every turn a pause, a reversal.

In the Master's Words
"A true picture book tells a story mainly or entirely with pictures. When words are used, they have an auxiliary role. The pictures provide information not contained in the words; and the words provide information not contained in the pictures." — Uri Shulevitz, Writing with Pictures (1985)

Shulevitz, a Caldecott-winning author-illustrator, wrote the field's standard craft text. His rule is a division of labor: two channels, no overlap.

Why It Works

The word is counterpoint: word and image don't sing the same line in unison—they run as two voices, and in the reader's mind they fuse into a third thing. That's where meaning is born. The most famous spreads in Sendak's Where the Wild Things Are—the three wordless "wild rumpus" pages—go silent exactly when the image peaks. This rule transfers in one step to slide design (echoing Day 6, Presentation Zen): don't just read the words on your slide aloud—let the image hold the visual, and let your mouth supply the half it can't. Image and text that repeat waste both.

Picture says bear at the door,
tears on its face

=

Counterpoint: two voices, no overlap—meaning fuses inside the reader
Before → After
(Illustration already shows the bear crying at the door.) The bear was very sad. It sat at the door and tears rolled down, drop by drop. (Same picture.) No one came. The bear counted the steps by the door: one, two, three… (The text never touches the tears—it writes the waiting, the half the picture can't hold.)
(Illustration shows a huge brown dog.) The dog was very big and brown and had long floppy ears. (Same illustration.) Nobody had ever told the dog he was too big for the couch. So every night, he tried anyway.
Where to Use It · Common Mistakes
  • ✓ Picture books, infographics, image-plus-caption posts—anywhere image and text sit side by side
  • ✓ Transfer: slides (don't read the screen), illustrated docs, product screenshots with copy
  • Trap: the text restates the picture—half the spread doing duplicate work
  • Trap: piled-up adjectives instead of action—"big and brown and cute" is weaker than letting it do one thing
This Week's Exercise · A Question

Open a picture book you (or your child) love. Cover the pictures, read only the words: does it make sense? Now cover the words, read only the pictures: is the story still there? A good picture book is "missing half" both times—the other half is what the partner supplies. Question: Last time you built slides, were words and image in counterpoint or singing in unison? Delete half the on-screen text—does your telling get stronger or collapse?

Principle 04

Reverse-Engineering the Classics: Two Layers Under a Simple Surface

Reverse-Engineering the Classics — Two Layers, One Truth
Dual Audience · Read Aloud
The Principle

The children's books that last run on two levels at once: a surface simple enough for the child, and a deep current underneath that strikes the adult reading it aloud. Charlotte's Web is a pig and a spider on top, death and friendship below; Where the Wild Things Are is mischief on top, "after the anger, still loved" below. Reverse-engineer a classic and you learn not the surface tricks—personification, rhyme—but why it survives rereading.

In the Master's Words
"There are some themes, some subjects, too large for adult fiction; they can only be dealt with adequately in a children's book." — Philip Pullman, Carnegie Medal acceptance speech (1996)

Pullman wrote His Dark Materials. Don't take "children's book" as a synonym for "shallow": the heaviest things often need the simplest form to bear them.

Why It Works

Classics survive on three things. One, read-aloud rhythm—children's books are meant to be spoken, so the sentences breathe and beat (echoing Day 42, Writing for the Ear). Two, repetition and escalation—a recurring pattern gives the child safety while quietly raising the stakes each time, building to a peak. Three, one true emotion at the core, unfaked and never condescending. This "minimal surface + true depth" is precisely the mark of all master-level expression: not childish, but complexity boiled down until only the essential drop remains. If your one-pager can satisfy the newcomer and the expert at once, it's the same move.

Before → After
(Trying to say "I love you," flat) I love you so, so much—more than anything. "I love you right up to the moon," said Little Nutbrown Hare.
Big Nutbrown Hare gathered him in: "I love you right up to the moon—and back." (From Guess How Much I Love You: measurable, picturable, and it leaves a return trip.)
That night Max was sent to his room, and he began to imagine a jungle. That very night in Max's room a forest grew—and grew—and grew—until his ceiling hung with vines. (From Where the Wild Things Are: repetition escalates, and the dashes let the sentence grow with the forest.)
Where to Use It · Common Mistakes
  • ✓ Studying any text that survives rereading—children's books, poems, one-liners, taglines—to see why "simple" isn't "shallow"
  • ✓ Transfer: writing a one-pager, opener, or product line that serves both newcomer and expert
  • Trap: copying the surface (personification, rhyme, cuteness) but missing the core—one true emotion—so it's all form
  • Trap: underrating the child (or the reader), turning "simple" into "babyish"—it shows on the first read
This Week's Exercise · A Question

Take a classic children's book you've reread and dissect three layers: (1) rhythm—which sentences are built to be read aloud? (2) repetition—how does it escalate within the repeats? (3) core—what's the one true emotion underneath? Write one sentence for each. Question: Could you use that "simple surface, deep undercurrent" structure on your next exec communication—letting the outsider read the surface and the insider read the current?

Going Deeper

Does the clarity trained by "writing for children" transfer straight to "writing for execs and engineers"? Where's the edge?
What transfers is the underlying craft: concrete nouns, short sentences, visible images, reverence for the reader's cognitive load—universal across readers. What doesn't carry over is register and emotional strategy: with a child you can lean on exaggeration, personification, and repetition to carry feeling; do that to a board and it reads as false. The one rule that truly transfers is "write up, not down"—whether the reader is six or a CEO, never condescend, never spin; make the complex land in one hearing. The difference is the vessel, not the standard of clarity.
Where do Chinese and English differ in the difficulty of "age-matching"?
English age-grading turns on word frequency and phonics: early readers use high-frequency short words and decodable spellings, and lean on Dr. Seuss-style rhyme and repetition to help kids read aloud. Chinese has no phonics ladder; the constraint is character count and abstraction—"高兴" (happy) is younger than "愉悦" (delighted), "怕" (afraid) younger than "恐惧" (fear). Chinese also has a private tool: reduplication and onomatopoeia (毛茸茸 "fluffy," 扑通 "plop")—used well it's a warm rhythm, overused it's cloying. One iron rule holds for both: read it aloud when you finish; a sentence that won't say smoothly, a child can't catch.
Does refusing to preach at all risk letting the value miss? Is that in tension with a memo that must state a recommendation?
No tension—they serve different ends. Values must be believed, which runs on experience, not command: preach and the reader resists; dramatize and they take it as their own discovery, and it internalizes. Decisions must be executed, which runs on clarity, not subtlety: burying the recommendation in a memo is a dereliction. The dividing line: do you want the reader to endorse a value (use story, skip the label) or take an action (say it straight, BLUF)? Good leadership writing does both: story to make them believe the "why," one sentence to state the "what."
AI can now mass-produce children's stories. Is human writing for children still needed?
AI generates fluent, rhyming, well-structured stories—it supplies the surface: rhythm, personification, cuteness. What it lacks is exactly Card 4's core: one true emotion, and the judgment of "what's worth saying to a child." Charlotte's Web moves us not because it's tidy but because White genuinely faced death without flinching or sugaring it. AI doesn't know which page should go silent and hand the stage to the picture, and it doesn't know what your child is afraid of tonight. The more it mass-produces a competent surface, the scarcer the person who can write a true core becomes.
